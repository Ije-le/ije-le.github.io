The Weight of Distance

How a generation of rural students fell through the cracks of a system designed, on paper, to catch them.

By Your Name  ·  April 29, 2026

On a Tuesday in March, Darnell Watkins drove 47 minutes to get to a school he had already left. He wasn't looking for a diploma. He was looking for a transcript — a single sheet of paper that would let him apply for a job at the county's new distribution center. The school's front office told him to come back Thursday. He couldn't. He worked Thursdays.

"We built systems for the average student in the average county. Out here, there is no average."

Across Maryland's five westernmost counties, a pattern has emerged in state discipline data that administrators have quietly acknowledged for years but rarely discussed publicly: students who are suspended once are dramatically more likely to disengage entirely. In Garrett County, that disengagement rate runs nearly 30 percentage points above the state median — a gap that has persisted, largely unchanged, through three consecutive governors' education reform packages.

The data, obtained through a series of public records requests filed over nine months, spans six school years and covers every local education agency in the state. It shows not just who gets suspended, but what happens next — re-enrollment rates, credit accumulation, and eventual graduation or dropout. For students in rural LEAs, the trajectory after a first suspension is measurably and consistently worse than for their suburban counterparts, even after controlling for poverty rates and prior academic performance.

State officials say the disparities reflect resource constraints that predate the current administration. "We can't staff a restorative practices coordinator in a district of eight hundred students the same way we do in a district of eighty thousand," said one senior official at the Maryland State Department of Education, who spoke on background because they were not authorized to comment on pending policy. "The math doesn't work."

But advocates argue that the math has never been made to work — that rural students have been excluded from the political calculus that drives education reform in Annapolis, because they have no lobby, no organizing infrastructure, and no geographic proximity to the legislators who write the budgets. The result, they say, is a structural inequity that compounds quietly, year after year, until it shows up in a county's workforce data, its poverty statistics, its empty storefronts.

Darnell eventually got his transcript. It took four visits and a call to a guidance counselor he'd had in ninth grade, who was now working half-time as a substitute. He got the job. Most of the other students whose records appear in these databases weren't so persistent — or so lucky.
