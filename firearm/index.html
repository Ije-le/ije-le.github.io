FIREARM DEATHS IN MARYLAND

Firearm homicides are dipping in Maryland, but gun suicides are not

Homicides and suicides involving firearms in Maryland declined after COVID, but in 2024, gun suicides began a slow climb.

By IJEOMA OPARA May, 2026

COLLEGE PARK, Md. – On Wednesday, April 2, 2025, Montgomery County Department of Police dispatched officials to a building in Chevy Chase, where a married couple lay dead with gunshot wounds.

The police published a news release five days later, saying officials found a gun and ammunition nearby, and the bodies had been transported to the chief medical examiner in Baltimore for autopsy.

"The manner of death for Kate Simoni Fralin was determined to be a homicide,” the police noted in the release. “While the official cause and manner of death for William Scott Fralin are pending, his death is being investigated as a suicide."

Shiera Goff, director of public information for the police department, told Capital News Service on Tuesday, April 21, that the case is now closed and was ruled as a murder-suicide.

Like the Fralin couple, nearly 5,400 people in Maryland have lost their lives to homicides and suicides involving firearms between 2018 and 2024, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

Firearm-related suicides and homicides in the state spiked during COVID and stayed high throughout 2021. In 2022, the figures started to drop. But while homicides continued to decline, suicides resumed the climb in 2024.

Preliminary data from Maryland’s department of health shows that this trend persisted in 2025.

It also reflects a nationwide pattern, where suicides are rising while homicides have fallen multiple years in a row.

Maryland’s urban jurisdictions –Baltimore County, Prince George’s, Anne Arundel, Baltimore City, and Montgomery– had the top five counts of firearm-related homicides and suicides between 2018 and 2024, according to a Capital News Service analysis of CDC data.

Baltimore city, Prince George’s and Baltimore county also had the highest firearm homicide rates per 100,000 people.

Firearm suicide rates were highest in rural jurisdictions where populations are typically smaller, with Garrett county topping the list at 12.9% per 100,000, even though counts were far lower than urban areas.

The CDC suppressed figures for counties where the number of deaths were less than 10.

While most rural counties ranked low in homicide rates, Dorchester had the same rate as Baltimore county, recording 7.9 homicide deaths per 100,000 and sharing third place. It also had the second highest rate of total suicides between 2018 and 2024.

Maryland legislators have taken steps to combat gun violence in the state, including passing strict firearm legislation and establishing intervention programs.

But Assistant Professor at University of Maryland’s Department of Criminology and Criminal Justice Zubin Jelveh said firearm policies often focus more on homicides than suicides.

“When firearm suicide is discussed, if ever it is, it is typically sort of framed as a mental health issue,” he said.

He identified deindustrialization as a contributing factor to rising suicide numbers. While the problem has been present in urban areas for longer, rural areas are more recently being confronted by the consequences, driving up rates of despair-related deaths.

“You could argue that the inner cities are more resilient, or maybe the problem has been around long enough such that they’ve been able to form some resiliency,” he said..
